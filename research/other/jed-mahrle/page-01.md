RESEARCH NOTES:
————————————————————

Subject line: [First_Name] and [First_Name] + [Company] reportedly performed well when no stronger contextual subject line existed.

Offer specificity example:

Bad:
"We'll audit your outbound strategy"

Good:
"We'll send you a 10-page report breaking down every gap in your current outbound process based on our work with over 20 [Niche] teams"

Secondary CTA examples:

"Or if you'd rather I just send a short video instead, I can do that."

"Happy to send a couple examples of companies like yours we've worked with, just let me know."

"Or would it make more sense to connect with [Colleague]?"

"I'm sure you've got a lot of competing priorities, if this isn't relevant right now want me to check back in a few months in case the timing changes?"

Cold-email acknowledgment example:

"I know you get a lot of emails like this"

Simple CTA example:

"Reply YES and I'll send it over."

Plain language example:

Instead of:
"We leverage our proprietary methodology to help revenue teams optimize their outbound motion."

Use:
"We help sales teams book more meetings from cold email."

--

KEY TAKEAWAYS
————————————————————

Specific offers outperform vague offers.

Secondary CTAs create easy reply paths.

Acknowledging it's a cold email can reduce resistance.

Strong offers allow extremely short emails.

Simple language beats corporate language.

Most of the article should be skipped because it's campaign strategy, segmentation, volume, signals, and outbound operations—not messaging assets.
